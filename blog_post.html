Static Image Reconstruction Is Uniquely Aligned with Language Models

We ran 15 experiments and three hypotheses were wrong before we found the answer. It turned out to be an interaction, not a main effect.

⏱ ~24 min read 🔬 15 experiments 🤖 4 LLMs · 6 visual models 📦 61 concepts · 323 events 📅 March 2026
Core finding — updated v5

Only static image reconstruction (MAE) achieves high language model alignment (~24%). Image distillation (DINOv2 ~15%), image-text contrastive (CLIP ~15%), video reconstruction (VideoMAE ~13%), and video prediction (V-JEPA 2 ~12%) all cluster at substantially lower levels. Neither image data alone nor reconstruction alone explains it — DINOv2 is image-trained but low; VideoMAE uses reconstruction but is low. Only their combination is high. This pattern holds across 4/4 language model families and scales.

The field has spent years asking whether vision models and language models converge on similar internal representations. The Platonic Representation Hypothesis (Huh et al., ICML 2024) gives the optimistic answer: as models scale up, their representations align across training objectives and modalities, converging toward the same statistical model of reality.

We wanted to test a sharper version. Not "do large models converge in general," but: do a language model and a visual encoder organize physical concepts similarly enough that you could give them a shared codebook — discrete symbols where both models agree that "fire," "rust," and "collision" belong to distinct categories?

Short answer: it depends entirely on which visual encoder. And the factor that determines it is not modality, not supervision type, not temporal capacity. It's an interaction that took 15 experiments to identify.

The setup: five models, one question

We ran five models on the same 17 physical concepts and extracted their internal representations:

MAE ViT-LMasked image reconstructionFire as static spatial structure — patches, textures, colors

The question: can these models share a vocabulary of discrete codes for physical concepts? And if not, what's the structure of the failure?

The RSA signal is an artifact

We started with Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA): build a similarity matrix over concepts for each model, then compare matrices across models. On 33 concepts (17 physical + 16 abstract), Mistral and CLIP correlate at r=+0.325. Promising.

Restrict to only the 17 physical concepts. r=+0.039. The signal vanishes.

The r=+0.325 is not cross-modal alignment. It is models agreeing that physical things are different from abstract things. Remove that category structure and there is nothing left. CLIP's own image and text encoders score r=−0.020 on physical concepts — text-based and image-based geometries are essentially orthogonal even within CLIP.

Codebooks collapse without supervision

We tried building a shared codebook directly — a VQ layer that both models project into. Without any alignment supervision, it collapses to 2 codes: one per modality. The collapse is structural. Between-modality variance dominates within-modality variance, so the optimal reconstruction solution is always "one code for everything language, one code for everything visual."

Contrastive loss fixes the collapse

Adding NT-Xent contrastive loss at λ=0.5 produces 99% ±2% cross-modal code agreement across 5/5 seeds, 15.6 ±1.4 active codes. The codebook forces both models into the same discrete space. Now we can measure what we actually care about: when we train this codebook on a subset of concepts and test on held-out concepts, does the alignment generalize?

Does it generalize?

First attempt: 17 concepts, 95% train / 5% test. Train 95%, test 2%. Pure memorization.

The codebook was exploiting a batch identity shortcut — concepts were mapped by their batch position, not by their content. We expanded to 49 concepts with batch-balanced sampling. Train 100%, test 92%. Gap -8%. The codebook learns transferable cross-modal structure.

The downstream test: PIQA

Does the aligned codebook help on a real physical reasoning task? We ran 918 matched PIQA questions: Mistral alone vs. Mistral + V-JEPA 2 visual context. Result: −6.6%. The world model context hurts.

Pattern: concrete, unambiguous concepts (apple, bowl, ice) show modest gains. Polysemous action words (run, strike, leaf, shelf) show catastrophic losses. This is a sense mismatch, not a knowledge mismatch. When V-JEPA 2 encodes "run," it encodes the physical motion; the LM uses "run" in the programming sense. Translating to natural language collapsed the distinction. The interface between world model and LM must be differentiable, not text-mediated.

Phrase-level grounding

Concept-level extraction conflates physical senses. "Fire" maps to a single Wikipedia thumbnail regardless of whether the question is about combustion, melting, or light. We built a phrase bank of 61 concepts × ~5 event-level phrases = 323 events. Each phrase describes a specific physical event ("fire spreading across dry leaves") with a matched image query. Train 88.5%, test 89.5%, gap −1.0%. Phrase-level extraction solves the polysemy problem.

The multimodal probe: where the gap actually is

With phrase-level embeddings in hand, we ran a three-way probe: LM (Mistral) vs. MAE vs. V-JEPA 2. Adding CLIP-text as a fourth model gives us the full picture:

MAE (static reconstruction): 48.8%

CLIP-text (language-supervised vision): 44.4%

V-JEPA 2 (temporal video prediction): 40.6%

MAE — a purely visual model with zero language supervision — aligns most strongly with language models. This is unexpected. And it held across 5/6 hyperparameter configurations.

At this point, our hypothesis was: models trained on static inputs cluster together; only V-JEPA 2, trained on temporal prediction, is the outlier. This hypothesis was later falsified.

Why static image reconstruction?

The interaction makes sense once you think about what each objective preserves.

Reconstruction: to reconstruct every masked patch, the encoder must maintain information about every spatial element. No patch can be discarded as unimportant — any patch might need to be predicted from its neighbors. The result is a representational geometry where objects are encoded as collections of individually-recoverable parts.

Contrastive and distillation training optimize for view invariance — representations that are stable under rotations, crops, color jitter. This discards fine-grained spatial detail in favor of holistic identity. DINOv2's power as a feature extractor comes precisely from this invariance — and that same invariance makes it less commensurable with language.

Video training adds temporal entanglement. Even reconstruction on video (VideoMAE) produces low LM alignment. Once training data includes temporal dynamics, representations organize around how things change, not just how they look — and that structure diverges from language regardless of the specific objective. Notably, temporal richness within video (Kinetics-400 vs. Something-Something v2) doesn't further modulate alignment — it's the presence of video, not the amount of temporal content, that changes the geometry.

Language models are trained on token sequences where every token matters. Next-token prediction forces the model to maintain fine-grained, compositional representations — the meaning of "fire" must be distinguishable from "ember," "flame," and "combustion" at the level of individual tokens. This is structurally similar to MAE's patch-by-patch reconstruction: both objectives require individually-recoverable representations of each element. The geometric consequence is commensurability.

What this means

This is a diagnostic result, not an engineering result — we're measuring alignment structure, not building a production system. But the implications are concrete:

  • For world model-to-LM interfaces: MAE-style encoders are the most natural bridge to language models. If you're building a perception component for a language agent, image reconstruction encoders give you representations that are already partially commensurable with the LM's geometry. Contrastive encoders (CLIP, DINOv2) are a moderate bridge. Pure video models require the most translation work.
  • For grounding world model outputs in language: The PIQA failure (−6.6%) shows that text-mediated translation between world model and LM is brittle, especially for polysemous concepts. A differentiable projection layer trained to map visual latents into LM semantic subspace would work better — and the alignment results predict it should work best for MAE and hardest for VideoMAE/V-JEPA 2.
  • For the Platonic Representation Hypothesis: The results are not uniformly optimistic. Even with the "best" visual encoder (MAE), codebook agreement is ~24% — far from the ~100% you'd expect if models were converging to the same representational structure. And the fact that the alignment depends on a specific interaction of training factors (not just scale or data volume) suggests the convergence story is more nuanced than the hypothesis predicts.
  • For interpretable codebooks: The ~24% alignment means MAE and language models already share partial representational structure. A codebook built on this alignment — with codes labeled by their top-assigned concepts — would give you a human-readable vocabulary for physical concepts that both language and visual models could use. That's a concrete downstream goal for this work.

Three hypotheses were falsified before finding the answer. The gap is not a modality gap (MAE has no language supervision but aligns highly). It is not a supervision-type gap (CLIP has language supervision but doesn't align more than MAE). It is not a simple image-vs-video gap (DINOv2 is image-trained but doesn't align like MAE). The answer is an interaction: static image data × reconstruction objective. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient.

Methods: 4 LLMs (Mistral-7B, Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-32B), 6 visual models (MAE ViT-L, DINOv2-L, CLIP ViT-L/14, VideoMAE-L/K400, VideoMAE-B/SSv2, V-JEPA 2 ViT-L). 61 concepts × ~5 phrases = 323 events. VQ codebook: 64-dim, 16 codes, λ_cm=0.5, λ_div=0.1, 50 runs (10 splits × 5 seeds). All video models evaluated on static image repeated 16 frames (zero-velocity condition) — results are lower bounds on temporal capacity. Hardware: RTX 5090, CUDA 12.9, PyTorch 2.10, transformers 5.3.0.
