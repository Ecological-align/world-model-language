Can a World Model and a Language Model Share a Vocabulary?

7 experiments 5 models 17 physical concepts ~15 min read

The field has spent years asking whether vision models and language models converge on similar internal representations. The Platonic Representation Hypothesis (Huh et al., ICML 2024) gives the optimistic answer: as models scale up, their representations align — different training objectives, different data, but ultimately converging toward the same statistical model of reality.

We wanted to test a sharper version of this. Not "do large models converge in general," but: do a language model and a world model — a model trained to predict what happens next in video — organize physical concepts similarly enough that you could give them a shared codebook and have them agree on a symbol for "fire"?

Short answer: not automatically, but you can make it work with the right supervision. And the two concepts that stubbornly refuse to align tell you something genuinely interesting about the limits of any such alignment.

The setup: five models, 17 physical concepts

We ran five models on the same 17 physical concepts — apple, chair, water, fire, stone, rope, door, container, shadow, mirror, knife, wheel, hand, wall, hole, bridge, ladder — and extracted their internal representations. The models are genuinely different kinds of thing:

Language
Mistral 7B / ST
Trained on text. Sees "fire" as a word with semantic neighborhood: combustion, danger, warmth, mythology.
Vision-Language
CLIP ViT-L/14
Trained on image-caption pairs. Sees fire as a visual object paired with how humans describe it.
World Model
V-JEPA 2
Trained to predict masked video regions. Sees fire as a dynamic process — how it moves, spreads, flickers.

The question is whether these different kinds of knowledge about the same concepts produce similar representational geometries — do the models agree on which concepts are similar to which other concepts?

The cross-modal RSA signal is an artifact

We used Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA): for each model, build a similarity matrix (how similar is fire to water? how similar is chair to knife?), then compare matrices across models. If two models organize concepts similarly, their matrices should correlate.

On a 33-concept set (17 physical + 16 abstract concepts like love, justice), Mistral and CLIP correlate at r=+0.325 — statistically significant, looks like a meaningful cross-modal alignment signal.

Restrict to only the 17 physical concepts, and it disappears entirely:

Model pair r (all 33 concepts) r (physical only, 17) Verdict
Mistral vs CLIP +0.325* +0.039 Artifact
ST vs CLIP +0.013 +0.089 No alignment
Mistral vs ST +0.148* +0.171 Marginal, both LMs

The r=+0.325 signal is not cross-modal concept alignment. It is the models agreeing that physical things are different from abstract things. Remove that category structure and there is nothing left.

This is a methodological point worth taking seriously. Prior work reporting cross-modal RSA correlations without within-category controls may be detecting category membership, not representational structure. The difference matters: category agreement tells you the models have the same coarse ontology; within-category agreement would tell you they actually organize the physical world similarly. The latter is what you need for shared representations to be meaningful.

Training objective, not architecture, is what matters

We added V-JEPA 2 (temporal video prediction) and MAE (masked image reconstruction) to the analysis. Both are ViT-L models with identical architectures — same parameter count, same dimensionality, same general training procedure. They differ only in what they were trained to predict.

Pair r p
V-JEPA 2 vs CLIP +0.404 0.005 ✓ significant
MAE vs CLIP +0.087 0.272 ✗ not significant
V-JEPA 2 vs Mistral −0.036 0.650 ✗
V-JEPA 2 vs ST −0.025 0.757 ✗

V-JEPA 2 aligns with CLIP; MAE doesn't. The architecture is identical, so architecture isn't the explanation. The distinguishing factor is V-JEPA 2's temporal prediction objective: to predict what a masked region of video will look like, the model must organize concepts around how they behave over time — their dynamics, their characteristic motion patterns. CLIP partially captures this through natural language supervision that includes captions describing action and motion.

MAE, by contrast, is trained to reconstruct masked image pixels — a purely spatial task that doesn't require understanding dynamics. Its representations organize concepts around appearance, not behavior.

The broader implication: the axis that matters for cross-modal alignment isn't "visual vs. linguistic." It's something like prediction over time vs. static reconstruction. A world model and a vision-language model can align because they both, in different ways, care about physical dynamics.

The codebook collapse problem

Given that V-JEPA 2 and CLIP partially align, can we build a shared codebook — a set of discrete symbols that all three types of models can use to represent the same concepts?

The architecture is straightforward: linear encoders project each model's representations into a shared 256-dimensional space, an EMA VQ layer (64 codes × 256 dimensions) quantizes them, and linear decoders reconstruct the originals. Train to minimize reconstruction error.

Result across every configuration we tried — 2-way, 3-way, different language models — the same thing happens: total collapse to 2 codes. One code for everything linguistic, one code for everything visual. Cross-modal concept agreement: 0%.

Mistral's 17 physical concept embeddings have mean pairwise cosine similarity of 0.926 — they're nearly a single point in representation space. The geometric spread of the concepts within a modality is swamped by the distance between modalities. So the codebook, optimizing for reconstruction, does exactly what it should: assigns one code to "this is language" and one code to "this is visual."

We tried replacing Mistral with Sentence-Transformers, which has 8× more geometric spread (0.639 vs 0.074). Still collapsed to 2 codes. The collapse isn't a Mistral-specific quirk — it's structural. As long as between-modality variance dominates, the codebook will always find it more profitable to split on modality than on concept.

The fix: contrastive loss at λ=0.5

The solution is direct: add an NT-Xent contrastive loss on the pre-quantization projections. For each batch, the loss pulls representations of the same concept from different modalities toward each other in the shared space, while pushing different concepts apart.

At λ=0.5: 14 active codes, 88% cross-modal agreement, post-VQ RSA of +0.755 between ST and V-JEPA 2 — up from r≈0 with no contrastive loss.

A few things worth noting about this result:

λ=1.0 overshoots. With too much contrastive pressure, the codebook has 12 codes instead of 14, agreement drops to 71%, and within-modality structure preservation collapses — from r=0.46 at λ=0.5 to r=0.09. The contrastive loss at high weight flattens the geometry so aggressively that the codebook loses the fine-grained structure it was supposed to preserve.

Reconstruction quality improves with contrastive loss. This seems backwards — you'd expect the contrastive term to compete with reconstruction. The explanation: with 2 codes, reconstruction is terrible because each code has to represent 17 concepts as a single point. With 14 codes, each concept gets its own region and reconstruction becomes much easier. The contrastive loss, by preventing collapse, makes the codebook better at its own primary job.

The post-VQ RSA (+0.755) exceeds any pre-training alignment we observed. The best pre-training alignment across our 5 models was CLIP↔V-JEPA 2 at +0.404. The shared codebook, with contrastive alignment, produces better cross-modal structure than any of the pre-trained models achieve on their own.

The irreducible residual: hand and hole

At λ=0.5, 15 of 17 concepts get assigned the same code across all three modalities. Two don't: hand and hole. They resist alignment across every λ value we tested.

hand
V-JEPA 2 sees A moving, grasping, force-applying object with characteristic dynamics. Something that reaches, grabs, releases.
Language sees Polysemous: anatomical body part, clock hand, playing hand, handwriting, "a hand" as assistance.
hole
V-JEPA 2 sees Negative space defined by surrounding objects and their dynamics. An absence with a shape.
Language sees Context-dependent abstraction: black hole, pothole, loophole, hole in one. Almost purely relational.

The honest framing is that this is post-hoc: we observed the disagreement and then constructed an account. But these are exactly the two concepts you would predict, from first principles, to resist alignment — hand because of its sensorimotor richness and extreme polysemy in language, hole because it's fundamentally a figure-ground relationship in vision and a relational abstraction in language.

More importantly: this is not a failure. A codebook that forces hand to be the same symbol in a temporal prediction model and a language model would be destroying genuine information. The world model's concept of hand — as dynamics, as interaction — is not the same concept as the linguistic hand. Preserving that difference is correct behavior.

What this means

The most direct practical implication is for modular multimodal systems — architectures that try to combine a separately-trained language model and a separately-trained world model without joint training. If you want them to share a vocabulary, the shared vocabulary cannot be learned by reconstruction alone. You need explicit concept-level alignment supervision. The minimum viable architecture is: shared encoder → contrastive loss on pre-VQ projections → VQ → decoder.

The Platonic Representation Hypothesis makes a convergence claim that our results don't contradict but do bound. The convergence Huh et al. document is real, but it appears to operate at the category level (physical things vs. abstract things) and through explicitly cross-modal objectives (like CLIP's image-caption contrastive training). For small models without paired supervision, the within-category structure of physical concepts is essentially orthogonal across modalities. "Scaling drives convergence" is compatible with "small specialist models remain orthogonal."

The V-JEPA 2 / MAE comparison also clarifies what "visual model" means for representational alignment. It's not visual processing per se that produces alignment with CLIP — it's temporal prediction. A model that has to predict what happens next to a physical object organizes concepts around their behavior. A model that reconstructs pixel values doesn't. If you care about grounding language in physical dynamics, temporal prediction objectives are significantly more relevant than reconstruction objectives, regardless of architecture.

The two words that no amount of alignment pressure could reconcile — hand and hole — are probably telling us something real about what it means for a concept to have "physical knowledge" that language doesn't capture. That residual is worth taking seriously.

Caveats

Seventeen concepts is genuinely small. The RSA significance tests have limited power at that sample size, and the λ=0.5 optimality finding comes from a single run per value — with variance across seeds, the ordering might shift. The hand/hole finding needs to be prospectively validated against polysemy or sensorimotor norm scores rather than discovered post-hoc. And evaluating V-JEPA 2 on static images repeated 8× to simulate video is a known workaround with unknown effects on representational structure.

These are real limitations. The core findings — collapse without contrastive loss, recovery with it, the structural explanation for collapse — are robust. The interpretive claims need more work.

What to do next

The most important extension is pre-registering which concepts should resist alignment based on sensorimotor norm scores (Lynott et al., 2020) or WordNet polysemy counts, then running the experiment. If the predicted and observed residuals match, the hand/hole story becomes much stronger. The second priority is multiple seeds with error bars around the λ sweep. Both are doable without substantial additional compute.
