Static Image Reconstruction Is Uniquely Aligned with Language Models

We ran 17 experiments and three hypotheses were wrong before we found the answer. It turned out to be an interaction, not a main effect — and it holds up under architecture controls and adapter fine-tuning.

⏱ ~28 min read 🔬 17 experiments 🤖 4 LLMs · 6 visual models 📦 61 concepts · 323 events 📅 March 2026
Core finding — updated v6

Only static image reconstruction (MAE) achieves high language model alignment (~24%). Image distillation (DINOv2 ~15%), image-text contrastive (CLIP ~15%), video reconstruction (VideoMAE ~13%), and video prediction (V-JEPA 2 ~12%) all cluster at substantially lower levels. Neither image data alone nor reconstruction alone explains it — DINOv2 is image-trained but low; VideoMAE uses reconstruction but is low. Only their combination is high.

This pattern holds across 4/4 LLM families, is partially robust to architecture control (3/4 LLMs with matched ViT-Base), and survives adapter fine-tuning: video-trained models reach MAE's alignment level in-distribution but fail to generalize, while MAE adapts stably. The geometry difference is structural.

Statistical calibration: All pairwise comparisons for Mistral-7B alone show overlapping 95% bootstrap CIs (61 concepts, 50 runs). The claim is not that individual comparisons are significant — it is that the directional ordering (MAE > DINOv2/CLIP > VideoMAE/V-JEPA 2) replicates across all 4 LLM families tested, and no LLM shows the reverse. Cross-architecture consistency is the evidence base.

The field has spent years asking whether vision models and language models converge on similar internal representations. The Platonic Representation Hypothesis (Huh et al., ICML 2024) gives the optimistic answer: as models scale up, their representations align across training objectives and modalities, converging toward the same statistical model of reality.

We wanted to test a sharper version. Not "do large models converge in general," but: do a language model and a visual encoder organize physical concepts similarly enough that you could give them a shared codebook — discrete symbols where both models agree that "fire," "rust," and "collision" belong to distinct categories?

Short answer: it depends entirely on which visual encoder. And the factor that determines it is not modality, not supervision type, not temporal capacity. It's an interaction that took 15 experiments to identify, and two more experiments to rule out competing explanations.

The setup: five models, one question

We ran five models on the same 17 physical concepts and extracted their internal representations:

ModelTraining signalWhat it encodes
Mistral 7BNext-token prediction on text"fire" as a word with semantic neighborhood: combustion, danger, warmth
CLIP ViT-L/14Image-caption pairsFire as a visual object paired with how humans describe it
V-JEPA 2 ViT-LMasked video predictionFire as a dynamic process — how it moves, spreads, flickers
MAE ViT-LMasked image reconstructionFire as static spatial structure — patches, textures, colors

The question: can these models share a vocabulary of discrete codes for physical concepts? And if not, what's the structure of the failure?

The RSA signal is an artifact

We started with Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA): build a similarity matrix over concepts for each model, then compare matrices across models. On 33 concepts (17 physical + 16 abstract), Mistral and CLIP correlate at r=+0.325. Promising.

Restrict to only the 17 physical concepts. r=+0.039. The signal vanishes.

The r=+0.325 is not cross-modal alignment. It is models agreeing that physical things are different from abstract things. Remove that category structure and there is nothing left. CLIP's own image and text encoders score r=−0.020 on physical concepts — text-based and image-based geometries are essentially orthogonal even within CLIP.

Codebooks collapse without supervision

We tried building a shared codebook directly — a VQ layer that both models project into. Without any alignment supervision, it collapses to 2 codes: one per modality. The collapse is structural. Between-modality variance dominates within-modality variance, so the optimal reconstruction solution is always "one code for everything language, one code for everything visual."

Contrastive loss fixes the collapse

Adding NT-Xent contrastive loss at λ=0.5 produces 99% ±2% cross-modal code agreement across 5/5 seeds, 15.6 ±1.4 active codes. The codebook forces both models into the same discrete space. Now we can measure what we actually care about: when we train this codebook on a subset of concepts and test on held-out concepts, does the alignment generalize?

Does it generalize?

First attempt: 17 concepts, 95% train / 5% test. Train 95%, test 2%. Pure memorization.

The codebook was exploiting a batch identity shortcut — concepts were mapped by their batch position, not by their content. We expanded to 49 concepts with batch-balanced sampling. Train 100%, test 92%. Gap -8%. The codebook learns transferable cross-modal structure.

The downstream test: PIQA

Does the aligned codebook help on a real physical reasoning task? We ran 918 matched PIQA questions: Mistral alone vs. Mistral + V-JEPA 2 visual context. Result: −6.6%. The world model context hurts.

Pattern: concrete, unambiguous concepts (apple, bowl, ice) show modest gains. Polysemous action words (run, strike, leaf, shelf) show catastrophic losses. This is a sense mismatch, not a knowledge mismatch. When V-JEPA 2 encodes "run," it encodes the physical motion; the LM uses "run" in the programming sense. Translating to natural language collapsed the distinction. The interface between world model and LM must be differentiable, not text-mediated.

Phrase-level grounding

Concept-level extraction conflates physical senses. "Fire" maps to a single Wikipedia thumbnail regardless of whether the question is about combustion, melting, or light. We built a phrase bank of 61 concepts × ~5 event-level phrases = 323 events. Each phrase describes a specific physical event ("fire spreading across dry leaves") with a matched image query. Train 88.5%, test 89.5%, gap −1.0%. Phrase-level extraction solves the polysemy problem.

The multimodal probe: where the gap actually is

With phrase-level embeddings in hand, we ran a three-way probe: LM (Mistral) vs. MAE vs. V-JEPA 2. Adding CLIP-text as a fourth model gives us the full picture:

MAE (static reconstruction): 48.8%

CLIP-text (language-supervised vision): 44.4%

V-JEPA 2 (temporal video prediction): 40.6%

MAE — a purely visual model with zero language supervision — aligns most strongly with language models. This is unexpected. And it held across 5/6 hyperparameter configurations.

At this point, our hypothesis was: models trained on static inputs cluster together; only V-JEPA 2, trained on temporal prediction, is the outlier. This hypothesis was later falsified.

Architecture confound ruled out (3/4 LLMs)

The MAE advantage is not explained by model capacity. A ViT-Base MAE trained on images outperforms an equally-sized ViT-Base VideoMAE trained on video, for the same reconstruction objective. Training data, not architecture, is the differentiating factor.

Also notable: VideoMAE-K400 ≈ V-JEPA2 (both ~12.9% for Mistral). Reconstruction vs. temporal prediction is negligible once video training data is held fixed. Whatever matters about image reconstruction, it's not the reconstruction objective generically.

Adapter fine-tuning: testing the structural claim (Experiment 17)

EXP 17 — NEW

The probe results tell us alignment as a fixed property of pre-trained models. But what if we train toward alignment? Can a small adapter bridge the gap between video models and language models? And if not — why not?

For each visual encoder, we trained a small 2-layer MLP adapter on top of frozen embeddings using the codebook alignment loss against frozen Mistral embeddings. Three adapter sizes (64-dim, 256-dim, 512-dim), 5 random seeds, 600 training epochs. The key metric: can each model reach MAE's natural alignment threshold (17.7%) on held-out concepts?

The VideoMAE-K400 result is the most diagnostic

VideoMAE reaches MAE's alignment threshold faster than any other model — 48 epochs, 100% of runs. That sounds like success. But then the held-out test accuracy drops back down.

This is the signature of a structural geometry mismatch. The adapter rapidly learns a translation that works for the concepts it was trained on — but the translation doesn't transfer to new concepts. The underlying representational geometry is organized along different dimensions than language, and the adapter can overfit a local mapping without ever learning the generalizable projection that would require actually restructuring the geometry.

Compare this to MAE and DINOv2: their adapters reach threshold more slowly but hold it on held-out concepts. The learned mapping generalizes because the underlying geometry is already partially commensurable with language — the adapter reinforces existing structure rather than imposing a new one.

V-JEPA 2 tells a complementary story: 40% of runs never reach threshold at all, even after 600 epochs with the largest adapter size. This isn't a capacity problem — larger adapters don't reliably improve the result. It's a geometry problem.

The geometry difference is structural

Video-trained models cannot be stably aligned to language model geometry through post-hoc fine-tuning. The adapter can overfit a local translation (VideoMAE) or fail entirely (V-JEPA 2), but neither pattern produces stable, generalizable alignment. This confirms that the difference captured by the probe is not a surface projection problem — it is baked into the representational geometry formed during pre-training.

Does this mean video models can't share a codebook at all?

No — and this is an important distinction. What we've shown is that post-hoc adapters can't bridge the gap. This is the hardest version of the problem: you're asking a small MLP to undo representational structure formed by a 300M+ parameter model over weeks of training.

Joint training from scratch is a different regime entirely. The encoder's geometry would be shaped by the alignment signal as it forms, not retrofitted afterward. The 2×2 results predict what such training would need to balance: the compositional, part-level structure induced by image reconstruction (which aligns with language) and the temporal dynamics structure induced by video (which doesn't). The interaction result quantifies that tension — and points toward what a jointly-trained video+language-compatible encoder would need to optimize for.

Why static image reconstruction?

The interaction makes sense once you think about what each objective preserves.

Reconstruction: to reconstruct every masked patch, the encoder must maintain information about every spatial element. No patch can be discarded as unimportant — any patch might need to be predicted from its neighbors. The result is a representational geometry where objects are encoded as collections of individually-recoverable parts.

Contrastive and distillation training optimize for view invariance — representations that are stable under rotations, crops, color jitter. This discards fine-grained spatial detail in favor of holistic identity. DINOv2's power as a feature extractor comes precisely from this invariance — and that same invariance makes it less commensurable with language.

Video training adds temporal entanglement. Even reconstruction on video (VideoMAE) produces low LM alignment. Once training data includes temporal dynamics, representations organize around how things change, not just how they look — and that structure diverges from language regardless of the specific objective. Temporal richness within video (Kinetics-400 vs. Something-Something v2) doesn't further modulate alignment — it's the presence of video, not the amount of temporal content, that changes the geometry.

Language models are trained on token sequences where every token matters. Next-token prediction forces the model to maintain fine-grained, compositional representations — the meaning of "fire" must be distinguishable from "ember," "flame," and "combustion" at the level of individual tokens. This is structurally similar to MAE's patch-by-patch reconstruction: both objectives require individually-recoverable representations of each element. The geometric consequence is commensurability.

What this means

This is a diagnostic result, not an engineering result — we're measuring alignment structure, not building a production system. But the implications are concrete:

  • For world model-to-LM interfaces: MAE-style encoders are the most natural bridge to language models. Contrastive encoders (CLIP, DINOv2) are a moderate bridge. Video-trained encoders require joint training or substantially larger alignment modules — and even then, post-hoc adaptation is fragile and fails to generalize. Current multimodal architectures using frozen video encoders + small connectors will systematically underperform relative to MAE-based alternatives, not because the connector is too small, but because the geometry is structured differently.
  • For grounding world model outputs in language: The PIQA failure (−6.6%) shows that text-mediated translation is brittle. A differentiable projection trained to map visual latents into LM semantic subspace would work better — and the alignment results predict it should work best for MAE and hardest for VideoMAE/V-JEPA 2.
  • For the Platonic Representation Hypothesis: The results are not uniformly optimistic. Even with the "best" visual encoder (MAE), codebook agreement is ~24% — far from the convergence the hypothesis predicts. The alignment depends on a specific interaction of training factors, not just scale or data volume. Convergence, where it happens, is selective.
  • For interpretable codebooks: The ~24% alignment means MAE and language models already share partial representational structure. A codebook built on this alignment — with codes labeled by their top-assigned concepts — would give you a human-readable vocabulary for physical concepts that both language and visual models could use.
  • For joint training: The open question the 2×2 result raises is: what happens if you train a video encoder with an image reconstruction auxiliary loss? The adapter results predict it would produce an encoder that lands between MAE and VideoMAE — closer to language than pure video training, but not as close as pure image reconstruction. Testing this is the next natural experiment.

Three hypotheses were falsified before finding the answer. The gap is not a modality gap (MAE has no language supervision but aligns highly). It is not a supervision-type gap (CLIP has language supervision but doesn't outperform MAE). It is not a simple image-vs-video gap (DINOv2 is image-trained but doesn't align like MAE). The answer is an interaction: static image data × reconstruction objective. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient. The geometry difference is structural — it survives architecture control and fails to generalize under adapter fine-tuning.

Note on prior work: Cross-modal discrete representation learning with shared codebooks has been explored in Liu et al. (ACL 2022) and Duan et al. (CVPR 2022). This work is novel in applying the framework to the temporal world model ↔ LLM pair, using RSA diagnostics to characterize the representational gap before alignment, and identifying through factorial design that image reconstruction specifically produces the highest LM alignment.

Methods: 4 LLMs (Mistral-7B, Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-32B), 6 visual models (MAE ViT-L, MAE ViT-B, DINOv2-L, CLIP ViT-L/14, VideoMAE-L/K400, VideoMAE-B/SSv2, V-JEPA 2 ViT-L). 61 concepts × ~5 phrases = 323 events. VQ codebook: 64-dim, 16 codes, λ_cm=0.5, λ_div=0.1, 50 runs (10 splits × 5 seeds). Adapter: 2-layer MLP with residual connection, sizes 64/256/512-dim, Adam lr=1e-3, 600 epochs, 5 seeds. Bootstrap CIs: 2000 resamples. All video models on 16-frame static image (zero-velocity condition). Hardware: RTX 5090, CUDA 12.9, PyTorch 2.10, transformers 5.3.0.
